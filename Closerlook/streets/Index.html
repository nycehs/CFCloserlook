The road to safety: Environments matter

Street safety in public health data

You may have seen news coverage of the 2019 cyclist fatalities in NYC. The numbers are clear: 2019 was a bad year for cycling.

Vision Zero data shows that from 2009 to 2019, the 3-year average number of cyclist injuries increased by 17%.

New York’s streets are both a place for health promotion - and a site of health risk. Our data reflect this.

When we look at public health data sets for indicators of street safety, we can compare rates of bicycle and pedestrian injuries. And, we can look for patterns that emerge between injuries and factors that might affect them - that is, factors that make streets more or less safe for you.

Hospitalization data shows us rates of bicycle- and pedestrian-related emergency department visits and hospitalizations. In these data, we can see clearly higher rates in Brooklyn and Queens, with Manhattan having notably lower rates.

Comparing areas: a tale of two cities

What’s behind the very different rates across the five boroughs? Let’s compare cycling injuries in the borough with the lowest rate, Manhattan, to the one with the highest rate, Brooklyn.

Brooklyn has a higher rate of bicycle injury ED visits and hospitalizations.

One might expect cyclists to be more in danger in areas where there’s more traffic. However, Manhattan’s lower rates of bicycle ED visits and hospitalizations exist despite a higher traffic density - meaning we can’t reduce injury rates to differences in traffic volume.

full map

Cycling volume could play a role, too - but when we map cycling in New York City, we see that a larger percentage of Manhattan adults regularly bike. Given the fact that Manhattan adults bike at a slightly higher rate (19.5%) than Brooklyn adults (17.3%), we might expect the population’s injury rate to be slightly higher, too - but it’s not.

full map 3

In environmental public health, we don’t just look at health outcomes and behaviors. We also examine environmental factors that can contribute to those outcomes.

So what could be behind Manhattan’s lower rates of bicycle injuries? Bicycle network coverage: Manhattan has a much greater percentage of streets with bike lanes.

Overall, Manhattan has twice as many streets with bike lanes as Brooklyn does. This demonstrates two important concepts:

Environmental protection. In environmental health, we often think of things in terms of risk and protection: factors that can either make a certain health outcome more likely or less likely. Streets can either be a risk factor for injury and death, or they can be protective.

The data show that in areas of high traffic density, bicycle networks offer a protective factor to people on the streets. The data also show that in areas with lower traffic density, the streets - and drivers’ behavior - pose a significant risk to New Yorkers.

Safety in numbers. When it comes to traffic safety, the idea of “safety in numbers” comes from a study that found that “a motorist is less likely to collide with a person walking and bicycling if more people walk or bicycle”- this is likely because as the number of people walking and cycling increases, motorists become more attentive. This means that as the number of cyclists rises, we expect to see injury rates go down.

NYC’s data appear to bear this out: various data sources suggest that the cycling population has risen substantially. The Health Department’s Community Health Survey shows that from 2009 to 2017, the number of New Yorkers who cycled regularly increased from about 660,000 to over 1,000,000. Meanwhile, the Department of Transportation estimates that daily cyclist trips doubled from 2011 to 2017.

This doesn’t constitute a rigorous epidemiological analysis, and any traffic-related death is a preventable tragedy. However, these numbers suggest that the number of NYC cyclists is rising faster than the number of injuries - that, consistent with “safety in numbers,” the rate of cyclist injuries is falling.

Environment matters. Streets matter.

From a public health perspective, streets are important because they’re the location of several key issues. They can:

  • Promote physical activity (and help prevent chronic disease) - or inhibit it
  • Offer safety and injury prevention, a tool to reduce traffic-related injuries and deaths
  • Have significant environmental consequences: namely, emissions from personal automobiles.

Simply put, streets can either be a source of environmental protection, or a source of risk.

You may have seen news about 2019’s cyclist fatalities last year, thought that cycling is simply too dangerous for you, and reached for your car keys or a rideshare app. But the data don’t show that this is a safer decision.

A large body of research suggests that as more people choose bikes, cities become safer: the “safety in numbers” study is one. A study of a car ownership lottery in China found that new drivers’ health got worse immediately after starting to drive regularly. And, another study concluded that the health and societal benefits of cycling outweigh the risks, even when you include the risk of a traffic collision.

After all, there are risks to driving, too. No matter where you are, choosing to drive or rideshare instead of walking, driving, or taking public transportation contributes to both traffic danger and to worse air quality. Our own data shows that New York City’s air pollution kills an estimated 2,000 people every year (more data on air quality and health).

Because of these factors, there are a myriad of public health benefits that come with changing our transportation systems to prioritize the safest, most efficient modes of moving people around the system. They result in safer streets, cleaner air, more mobility, and healthier people - which is why it’s important to keep building street infrastructure that keeps people safe.

May is Bike Month, so we encourage you to grab that bike and take it for a spin around the neighborhood. Or get a bike share membership. Combine your commute with your workout. Call a friend and spend a day exploring a neighborhood you’ve never been to. Feel the joy of two wheels, if you can. The data don't lie - it’s not only good for you, it’s good for the whole city.
