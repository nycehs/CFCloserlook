The road to safety: Environments matter

Street safety in public health data

New York City’s streets are a place for both health promotion and health risk. They offer opportunities for active transportation like cycling and walking, but can also pose a risk from collisions. We had a stark reminder of that in 2019, as cyclists’ deaths were at an historic high of 28, up from a historic low of 10 the previous year.

We can look at public health data to understand how to maximize health promotion and minimize risk. Our data includes hospitalization and emergency room data, which tracks injuries to pedestrians and cyclists. Along with these injury statistics, we have data on traffic density, infrastructure, and cycling.

When we look at these data together, they suggest that the more extensive the infrastructure and the more cyclists there are, the safer streets are as an environment for physical activity.

A safety in numbers story emerges - let’s take a closer look.

Disparities in NYC's traffic-related injuries

When we look at data on traffic-related injuries by borough in New York City, Brooklyn and Queens show higher rates of hospitalization and ED visits, with Manhattan showing notably lower rates.

What’s behind the very different rates across the five boroughs? Let’s compare cycling injuries in the borough with the lowest rate, Manhattan, to the one with the highest rate, Brooklyn.

Brooklyn has a higher rate of bicycle injury ED visits and hospitalizations. Its rates are higher than the citywide averages.

What explains this difference?

One might expect cycling to carry higher injury risk in areas where there’s more traffic. However, Manhattan’s lower rates of bicycle ED visits and hospitalizations exist despite a higher traffic density - meaning we can’t reduce injury rates to differences in traffic volume.

Cycling volume could play a role, too. When we map cycling in New York City, we see that a larger percentage of Manhattan adults regularly bike. Given that Manhattan adults bike at a slightly higher rate (19.5%) than Brooklyn adults (17.3%), we might expect the population’s injury rate to be slightly higher - but, as we saw from the hospitalization data, it’s not.

In public health, we don’t just look at health outcomes and behaviors as influences on outcomes. We also examine environmental factors that can contribute to outcomes. Bicycle network coverage may be such a contributor. Manhattan has a much greater percentage of streets with bike lanes.

Overall, Manhattan has twice as many streets with bike lanes as Brooklyn does. The data suggest that in areas of high traffic density, bicycle networks offer a protective factor to people on the streets. The data also suggest that in areas with lower traffic density, the streets - and drivers’ behavior - pose a significant risk to New Yorkers.

Safety in numbers: Promoting health, reducing risk

The idea of “safety in numbers” comes from a study that found that “a motorist is less likely to collide with a person walking and bicycling if more people walk or bicycle.” This is probably because as the number of people walking and cycling increases, motorists become more attentive. So as the number of cyclists rises, we expect to see injury rates go down.

This means that ongoing work to promote cycling will help reduce injury rates. Expanding bike share networks, increasing bicycle network coverage, and continuing to build protective street environments - like separated bike lanes - can get more riders on the street and offer greater protection to them.

May is Bike Month

Under ordinary circumstances, we’d encourage you to grab your bike and take it for a spin around the neighborhood, buy a bike membership, try commuting to work, or call a friend and explore a neighborhood you’ve never been to. We’d want you to feel the joy of two wheels, if you can.

Under the threat of COVID-19, though, life is a little bit different now. If you have to move around the city, walking or biking - while keeping a safe distance from others - might be the best bet. Furthermore, safely doing so is important for our mental health as we look for ways to handle the stress of an outbreak and of our own isolation.

Outbreak or no, the data don’t lie - it’s good for you, and it’s good for the whole city.
