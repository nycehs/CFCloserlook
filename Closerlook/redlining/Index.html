How redlining explains health in NYC

In public health, we often acknowledge that structural injustice and systemic racism can explain health patterns in our society. But what, specifically, does this mean? A closer look at the history of redlining shows this connection.

When we analyze data on a health condition that affects New Yorkers, we often notice some recurring patterns: like disparities by neighborhood poverty level. For example, we've written about how asthma and poverty show a close connection.

If we add data on the percent of each neighborhood's population that are people of color, we see that neighborhoods with higher rates of poverty also have a larger proportion of people of color, and much higher rates of childhood asthma emergency department (ED) visits.

In the chart below, we see the relationship between each neighborhood's poverty rate and percent non-white population - as well as its relationship to the rate of childhood asthma ED visits, represented by the size of each bubble.

This dynamic extends beyond asthma to many areas of health. To understand why poverty, race, and health are related in New York City, we look at a government policy from the 1930s. When we do, we learn more about what poverty is, and what it isn't.

A brief history of redlining

In the 1930s, the Great Depression caused a wave of foreclosures throughout the country. Unemployment rates were high and many people couldn't make mortgage payments. To help keep people in their homes, the federal government established the Home Owners Loan Corporation (HOLC). Its goal was to refinance mortgages with better terms – less interest and a longer repayment times. This would help people make payments and avoid foreclosure.

However, to determine what loans they could safely guarantee, HOLC sent people to appraise neighborhoods in cities across the USA.

They documented the types of housing in neighborhoods. They also documented information about the people who lived in these neighborhoods. Worse, they catalogued “detrimental influences,” citing things like “infiltration of Negroes” and “mixed races" as things that lowered a neighborhood's value.



With this information, HOLC made “residential security maps” for almost 250 cities in the USA. On these maps, HOLC gave each neighborhood a classification - best, still desirable, definitely declining, or hazardous - to determine whether to guarantee loans. Banks used them to determine if people were eligible for mortgages. They wouldn't give loans for people to buy homes in the lower-ranked neighborhoods.

This was "redlining" - drawing boundaries around certain neighborhoods and depriving them of resources. The map below shows the extent of redlining in New York City. Huge residential areas were declared declining or hazardous.

This policy drove residential segregation

As a result of redlining, people of color were denied access to “desirable” neighborhoods by racist real estate practices - and prevented from buying property even in neighborhoods labeled "declining" or "hazardous." Meanwhile, real estate agents used exploitative tactics like blockbusting, contract selling, and racially restrictive covenants to deepen and enforce neighborhood segregation.

Asmany white people left previously integrated cities for newly-segregated suburbs, supported by this system, many people of color were barred from opportunities to get loans and buy property.

This was clearly racist. HOLC's neighborhood classifications were based on the race of people living in those neighborhoods. The redlining maps are a textbook case of "structural racism," when racism is built into the rules and foundations of society.

Owning a home is a powerful way to achieve financial stability. By preventing people of color from accessing loans and buying property, these policies prevented them from accruing wealth. The policies concentrated people of color in neighborhoods deprived of investment - effectively racializing poverty in cities across the USA.

In New York City, recent decades of reinvestment in some formerly-redlined neighborhoods in Manhattan and Brooklyn has somewhat broken the relationship between original redlining and today's poverty rates. However, this is in part due to the displacement of the original residents of those neighborhoods. Explore the extent of HOLC's redlining in NYC's five boroughs:

Large parts of Manhattan were redlined. However, reinvestment in recent decades has lead to rising property values and the displacement of some former residents. Neighborhoods that were once redlined now have some of the city's most expensive real estate.



As with Manhattan, huge swaths of residential neighborhoods in the Bronx were declared "declining" or "hazardous" in HOLC's redlining map. Unlike Manhattan, though, the Bronx has not seen the same level of reinvestment and redevelopment,and poverty rates remain high.



Brooklyn also saw extensive redlining that lead to deep disinvestment and generations of poverty.



Queens saw significantly less redlining than other boroughs.



Like Queens, Staten Island saw much less redlining than other boroughs.

Redlining's effects continue today

In New York City, many once-thriving neighborhoods experienced severe disinvestment as a result of redlining.

HOLC's redlining maps caused inequality that continued from one generation to the next: neighborhoods that were redlined in the 1930s have higher rates of poverty even today – nearly 90 years after the maps were created. According to the National Community Reinvestment Coalition, 3 out of every 4 neighborhoods in the USA that were redlined in the 1930s are still low-to-moderate income today - and 2 out of every 3 are predominantly populated by people of color.

Disinvestment threatens health

Poverty is a key determinant of health. The World Health Organization says that that poverty "forces people to live in environments that make them sick."

Redlining drove segregation and concentrated urban poverty. It's a prime example of neighborhood disinvestment: denying or withholding public and private funding, city services, or other resources that neighborhoods - and their residents - need to thrive. When these resources are withheld, it creates environments that threaten health.

Policies like redlining create deeply entrenched systems of inequality. They show us that poverty isn't a bit of bad luck, or when somebody can't find a good job, or has to work a little harder to make ends meet. Poverty is something deeper and more powerful: generations of deprivation and disinvestment that withholds resources, opportunity, wealth, and health from people and neighborhoods - often, along racial lines.

Though redlining was banned in 1968, other forms of housing discrimination persist. But redlining is special: because of how it worsened poverty and segretation in cities across the United States, and how disinvestment creates environments that threaten people's health, we now see a close connection between poverty and health, and racial disparities in health outcomes.

It reminds us that public health is about far more than changing people's behaviors at the individual level: it's about building a society that supports health and well-being, and moving into our future by confronting our past.
