A public health approach to preventing violence

Experiencing violence affects people in many ways. Of course, it can cause physical harm. It also causes emotional harm, inflicting psychological trauma and fear.

Violence doesn’t just affect the immediate victim, but affects their surrounding community, too, which may be torn apart by trauma and fear.

Yes, violence is a behavior that some people do – but we’re focused on how people experience violence: what predicts whether they do, and what happens as a result.

Violence affects people throughout their lives – it’s associated with worse birth outcomes, childhood development, physical and mental illness, and premature deaths.

By viewing violence as a health outcome, the Health Department shifts from a criminal justice narrative to a public health lens –where we use data and research to figure out what leads to violence, so we can prevent it before it happens.

How can we use data to understand and reduce violence in New York City?

Violence in New York City has a complicated history and many connected and varied causes. The Health Department is exploring new ways to reduce violence by strengthening the communities most affected by violence.

We are taking a public health approach to violence: analyzing evidence on factors that may explain or predict violence, and then taking action based on the evidence to address those root causes of violence.

NYC's homicide rate has plummetted since the 80s

There’s a lot less violence than there used to be in NYC.

Let’s look at homicides – the most extreme violence outcome. In recent years, NYC’s homicide rate has been under 5 per 100,000 people – down from a high of over 25 per 100,000 people from 1986-1990.

But this drop doesn’t tell the whole story.

How do we prevent violence in these neighborhoods?

About disinvestment
Disinvestment is when either government agencies or private companies deliverately withdraw resources and opportunity from a neighborhood or community. The history of disinvesment is tied to racist practices and policies - like preventing people of color from being able to access loans to buy property, or preventing them outright from buying property. It also looks like a steady decline of public funding for public housing, services, education - especially in cities, and especially in neighborhoods populated mostly by people of color.

To prevent violence, we need to explore what leads to it.

Decades of government and societal disinvestment has led to high poverty rates in some NYC neighborhoods.

Neighborhoods with higher poverty rates also experience higher rates of violence.

Disinvestment threatens health.

Disinvestment promotes segregation, limits people’s access to basic resources, and creates environments that threaten people’s health.

In terms of public health, we say that disinvestment creates environmental factors that make certain health outcomes more likely.

By “environmental,” we mean the entire physical, social, and economic context around us: the built environment, like housing and parks, and things like access to schools, jobs, opportunity, healthcare, and more.

Whether or not a neighborhood experiences more violence can be explained by looking at whether its environment has protective factors, which help prevent violence before it starts, or risk factors, which make violence a more likely outcome.

Protective factors include:

Child health and wellbeing
Economic stability
High quality education
Housing stability

Risk factors include:

Poverty, and child poverty
Economic instability
Housing instability
Experiencing trauma or violence

What does this mean for NYC's neighborhoods?

Due to this legacy of disinvestment, New York City neighborhoods experience these protective and risk factors very differently.

Some neighborhoods have a lot less of the protective factors – and a lot more of the risk factors.

Let’s look at two very different neighborhoods in our city.

Poverty ranges from 6.2% to 41.5% among NYC neighborhoods. Belmont/East Tremont, in the Bronx, has one of the highest poverty rates in the city.

Greenwich Village/SoHo, in Manhattan, has one of the lowest.

Neighborhood poverty is one of the environmental factors that predicts violence, with neighborhoods with higher rates of poverty more likely to also have high rates of non-fatal assault hospitalizations. Belmont/East Tremont also has a high rate of non-fatal assault hospitalizations - one of the highest in the city (107 per 100,000 people0.

Greenwich Village/SoHo follows the same pattern, with one of the lowest rates of non-fatal assault hospitalizations - so few that we suppress the rate for confidentiality.

These risk factors describe a network of environmental threats in communities that have experienced disinvestment. These threats place residents in poverty and expose them to risks throughout their lives – culminating in higher rates of violence.

A pattern is clear: neighborhoods with more violence have more risk factors that affect residents throughout their lives.

And neighborhoods with less violence? They experience far less risk – instead, experiencing protective factors that prevent violence. This chart looks at these factors for neighborhoods with higher poverty rates compared to neighborhoods with lower poverty rates:



When we look at these factors together, it shows that in environments where risk factors far outweigh protective factors, the scale tips – and higher rates of neighborhood violence emerge.

Why does this happen?

In this system, violence perpetuates itself: when violence happens to people, it makes them more likely to exhibit violent behaviors.

"Cycle" image" showing protective, risk, and violence

This means that there is a cycle of violence – which is a consequence of these major, systemic factors where risk outweighs protection in affected communities.

How deep do these systems go?

This redlining map was created 90 years ago as part of racist housing policies that set off generations of disinvestment.

A map of recent shootings lines up with the heavily redlined area of the Bronx, Harlem, and northern and eastern Brooklyn.

Swipe side to side map of redlining and shootings

Redlining created intergenerational, racialized poverty – the backbone for the health patterns we see today.

Community poverty isn’t about individual residents’ lack of success. Systematic disinvestment in specific neighborhoods results in a set of related factors that deprive people living in these areas of opportunity and resources.

But we can break the cycle...

In NYC, we are taking a public health approach to violence, working across systems and communities to reinvest in neighborhoods most affected by violence.

  • We’re doing and sharing research on the most effective policies to reduce violence
  • Through the Crisis Management System, the Health Department works with several other city organizations to support community-led efforts to de-escalate violence, providing support to at-risk youth with employment, legal, therapeutic, and educational opportunities.
  • We’re strengthening the Neighborhood Health Action Centers in the South Bronx, Harlem and Central Brooklyn, which all experience high levels of violence. The Action Centers serve as community hubs for programs and resources that decrease risk and increase protective factors.
  • We’re working with hospitals to help them respond to the many needs – physical, social, and emotional – of people injured by violence.
  • Partnering with communities to respond to neighborhood-level mental health crises and traumatic events through Rapid Assessment and Response.
With this work, we’re reinvesting in communities that have experienced disinvestment so that we can build environments where protective factors outweigh risk.

...and reduce rates of violence.

A public health approach to violence means that we explore the data and identify environmental risk factors that are associated with violence – like poverty, substandard housing, and the lack of educational and economic opportunities.

A public health approach to violence presents an opportunity to find sustainable solutions to create healthy spaces for New Yorkers to live and thrive.

It means that we can prevent violence “upstream” – working to prevent violence by eliminating poverty, providing jobs, healthy housing, and education: a society based on justice and opportunity.

Together we can break the cycle – and reduce rates of violence.
