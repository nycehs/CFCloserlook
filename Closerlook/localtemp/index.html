The Urban Heat Island Effect in New York City

At 6.00 PM on June 30, 2018, the air temperature at the La Guardia Airport Weather Station was 89° Fahrenheit.

At the same time, the air temperature on a block in East Harlem (below left) was nearly 5° hotter than at the LGA weather station, and the temperature on a block in Bed-Stuy (below right) was 3.5° hotter than at the weather station.

100th St, between Lexington and 3rd Ave
Manhattan: 93.9° F
Stuyvesant Ave, between Jefferson and Putnam
Brooklyn: 92.5° F

This is an example of the Urban Heat Island Effect: when cities are hotter than surrounding areas.

In cities, more surfaces like roofs and roads absorb heat, building boilers and car engines generate additional heat, and there's limited vegetation to cool things down. As the pictures above illustrate, temperatures even vary from block to block within a city.

What cools your block?

Characteristics of different areas contribute to temperature differences from one block to another.

Anyone who has looked for a shady spot under a tree during a hot sunny day knows that trees help to cool off a street. But by how much? How effective are trees at cooling neighborhoods, and what other characteristics contribute to an area’s air temperature during hot weather?

To find out how much different characteristics affect "hyperlocal temperature," we installed air temperature monitors on blocks around the city. Then, we reviewed land-use data and satellite imagery to build a profile of each block where we were monitoring temperature.

To measure characteristics of blocks where we were monitoring temperature, we looked at the right of way. That's the area from the front of one building, across the side walk and street, to the buildings on the other side of the street - or, between the blue lines on the image above.

Below are two exaples of locations where we measured temperature throughout the summer. On the left is a spot in Brownsville, Brooklyn. On the right is a spot in Edenwald, The Bronx.

These two blocks have dramatically different characteristics. The Brownsville location has very little vegetative material (trees, grass, and shrubs), while the Edenwald location has high grass and tree coverage. The average temperature at the Edenwald spot is over 2° cooler than the Brownsville spot.

These are the factors we explored, which affect hyperlocal temperature:

Tree canopy coverage

The percent of total tree canopy coverage on the sidewalk in the right of way of the monitored street. Trees provide shade and prevent the ground from heating up. They also can cool the air through evapotranspiration. Evapotranspiration happens when the sun’s rays hit plants, causing water to evaporate from the leaves. This change from liquid to vapor cools the plants – just as sweating cools our bodies – and uses energy thereby reducing the amount of energy left to warm the air.

Grass and shrubs

The percent of grass and shrubs on the sidewalk of the monitored street. Grass and shrubs cool the air through shading and evapotranspiration. Evapotranspiration happens when the sun’s rays hit plants, causing water to evaporate from the leaves. This change from liquid to vapor cools the plants – just as sweating cools our bodies – and uses energy thereby reducing the amount of energy left to warm the air.

Building height

Our model looks at the median building height (the middle range of sorted values) of buildings on the block. Taller buildings can absorb and reflect more heat (increasing the street's temperature), but also shade the street to cool it.

Impervious surfaces

Impervious surfaces are made from asphalt, concrete, astroturf, even hard packed dirt. These surfaces absorb sunlight and reflect and retain heat. They increase the temperature of the surrounding area.

Other factors

Other factors, like the orientation of the street (North/South or East/West), how close the area is to the ocean, the elevation of the area, daily wind speed and weather patterns can also influence how hot a block is. These other factors are hard to quantify reliably, but can affect variation in hyperlocal temperature.

What factors matter the most?

We built a statistical model predicts the average annual temperature on a hypothetical city block, based on these neighborhood factors. We modeled the average temperature from 6 to 9PM, when the Urban Heat Island Effect is the greatest (the sun sets but the city retains heat). Our model showed that the most important factor was green space: trees, grass, and shrubs reduce temperature the most.

This is intuitive. Imagine a block that has a park running down the middle of it instead of a street. The right of way is completely covered in trees, grass, or shrubs.

More greenspace means lower temperatures, and the model shows that grass and shrubs can provide substantial cooling - even more than tree canopy coverage.

But not many blocks in New York City have vegetation running down the middle. Most have a street. A street is a form of impervious surface. These surfaces are asphalt, concrete, or even hard packed dirt. They absorb sunlight and retain heat, and they take up room that could be covered in grass or shrubs.

So, we added impervious surfaces to the model, and combined tree canopy and grass and shrubs together as "vegetative material" to see how these impervious surfaces affect the predicted temperature.

A block where the right of way is covered in trees, grass, and shrubs might have an average summertime evening temperature of 77°, but one completely covered in impervious surfaces would be 79°.

Our models show the potential for significant cooling from vegetative material, but once we add impervious surfaces into the equation, we see the potential for only about 2° of cooling.

This may sound small, but...

Cooling a block by an average of 2° may not sound like much, but small differences in temperature - especially at peak summer temperatures - can mean the difference bewteen small numbers and large numbers of heat-related emergency department visits, and heat-related deaths.

After all, hot weather is dangerous. In New York City and across the country, more people die from extreme heat than from all other natural disasters combined. And, NYC is more vulnerable to heat than suburban areas.

That means that rising average temperatures and heat waves are even hotter in the city. These place New Yorkers at risk: when we analyzed excess mortality during extreme heat events, we estimated that over 110 New Yorkers died each year due to extreme heat (1997 – 2013).

When we distill the data to understand how these different factors affect temperature, the extent to which greenspace or tree canopy alone can cool a neighborhood may seem small – a few degrees. But this small change can have a significant impact: the risk for heat-related illness rises exponentially around 90°. Even modest cooling can save lives, especially as we anticipate increasing frequency and intensity of extreme heat events as the climate changes.
