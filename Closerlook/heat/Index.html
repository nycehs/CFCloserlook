Hot weather can kill

Hot weather is dangerous. In New York City and across the country, more people die from extreme heat in an ordinary year than from all other natural disasters.

Heat stress (also called heat stroke) is a preventable condition that happens when your body’s temperature increases quickly and it can no longer cool itself down. This can quickly lead to death. At the NYC Health Department, we track heat stress deaths by reviewing causes of death recorded by the Bureau of Vital Statistics. In NYC, the number of heat stress deaths vary considerably by year with more deaths usually happening during hotter summers. From 1999 to 2016, there were 4 such spikes due to severe heat emergencies: 1999, 2006, 2011, and 2013.

But deaths due to heat stress only tell part of the story. The effects of heat can be hard to recognize – heat can kill by worsening respiratory or cardiovascular illnesses, which may not be included on someone’s death certificate.

So to better understand the danger that hot weather poses to New Yorkers, we needed to dig deeper into the data. We examined the difference between the number of deaths during extreme heat and the average number of deaths during typical, non-extreme warm season days. That difference is called the excess mortality due to extreme heat.

In NYC, we saw that excess mortality increases during extreme heat events (2 or more days with a heat index over 95 degrees, or one day over 100 degrees). In fact, we found that the deaths that we can attribute to extreme heat events are nearly 10 times higher than the number of deaths where heat stress was recorded as the cause of death.

This gives us a clearer picture of the burden of extreme heat events in New York City.

What puts people at risk?

We investigated heat stress deaths by looking at medical examiner records. One environmental risk factor was clear. Of the 48 people who died from 2008-2011 whose records we examined, 41 became sick in their own homes.

What prevents heat-related illness at home? Air conditioning. Of the records with information on whether their home had air conditioning, none of those who died had a working air conditioner.

90% of households in NYC report having an air conditioner – but in some of NYC’s low-income neighborhoods, this drops to as low as only 75%.

Having an air conditioner is one thing – but using it is another. When we surveyed New Yorkers, we found many who often didn’t use an air conditioner despite having one – because they couldn’t afford the electricity bill.

The COVID-19 pandemic is likely to further worsen the financial hardship of people who are already disproportionately impacted by heat, making the use of air conditioning even more unaffordable - placing them at further risk.

Climate change is bringing more extreme heat

Access to air conditioning is an issue of climate equity. We anticipate more extreme heat events as our climate continues to warm – and so we’re working to protect some of New York City’s most vulnerable residents. A deep dive into the data on who is at risk allowed us to create the Heat Vulnerability Index, which scores neighborhoods based on surface temperature, lack of green space, and other factors that place people at risk, like poverty.

As we prepare for a changing climate, we need to view air conditioning not as a luxury, but more like we view heat in the winter: as something necessary to survival for those who are most at risk of heat’s deadly effects. At the same time, there must be responsible, non-excessive air conditioning use to ensure that people who need it to survive can access it, to protect our power grid and to reduce contributions to climate change itself.

One of the City’s strategies for keeping people safe from hot weather is offering air-conditioned places where people can gather to stay cool. But since the COVID-19 pandemic has made it dangerous to gather together, new strategies are needed to protect New Yorkers. People at high risk of severe outcomes from COVID-19 are also at high risk when it’s hot. To avoid compounding the inequitable effects of COVID-19 this summer, access to in-home cooling and community connectedness will be all the more important this summer.

A healthy indoor temperature should not be a privilege limited to those who can afford to own and use an air conditioner – especially when its consequences are deadly.
